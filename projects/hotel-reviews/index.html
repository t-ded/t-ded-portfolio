Project 1 Overview: Analysis of hotel reviews (Regression task)

September 1, 2020 - 10 minutes read - 2108 words

Analysis of more than 515 thousand reviews for European hotels - Project Overview:

Project Summary

  • Comprehensively explored and visualized the data, followed up by predicting score given by the reviewer with RMSE of 0.73 points on 1-10 review scale.
  • Engineered multiple features from the data, such as country of each hotel from address and length of stay from tags of each review.
  • Performed sentiment analysis on text for each review, managed to find the most impactful pairs of words and how they affected the reviewer.
  • Implemented Regressive Histogram Gradient Boosting, Linear Regression, and Random Forest algorithms, extracted feature importance and performed feature selection.
  • Might actually bring meaningful business implementation for a company in a related field (both the algorithms and findings of my EDA).
  • In the future, the continuous target variable may be transformed into an ordinal categorical one, enabling me to try classification algorithms.
  • Also, a clustering analysis may be performed onto the reviewers, giving us even more precious insight into their demands and behaviour.

Project Background

  • This dataset has been chosen as it revolves around a topic which is close to almost anyone; apart from that it offers a possibility of an impactful business implementation.
  • The initial dataset consists of 515 738 reviews and 34 features, one of them being the reviewer score which is the dependent variable.
  • For this project, EDA, visualisation and data interpretation have been given priority over algorithms as it seemed to me that for this particular dataset, it would be much easier to actually implement the results in the form of graphs, charts and plots into business, rather than estimator predictions and their coefficients.

Project Outline

  • State the initial hypotheses.
  • Make necessary imports, take first look at the dataset.
  • Extract country of each hotel from review and analyse hotel countries and reviewer nationalities.
  • Find the best and worst hotels, analyse these extremes in relation to country of each hotel.
  • Analysis of reviews in relation to time.
  • Dive into tags - length of stay, with whom, purpose of the trip etc.
  • Analyse reviews - positive, negative, lengths of each, sentiments.
  • Inspect correlation.
  • Implement models for regression tasks (multivariate linear regression, decision tree regressor, random forest reg.).

Initial Hypotheses

Let me start with some initial hypotheses around which the following analysis will revolve. I will be trying to either prove or disprove them and they will lead my path throughout the entire project. My hypotheses are the following:

  • Long negative review will correspond to worse rating (vice versa for positive).
  • Newer reviews of the same hotel will be better (improvements based on the reviews).
  • Reviewer nationality influences the average given score.
  • Some countries will have better hotels in general.
  • People will prefer to visit hotels in their country and will give them better rating.
  • Some words in positive/negative review will have big impact.
  • Some tags will have an impact.

Analysis breakdown

First steps

The beginnings of this project were nowhere away from the ordinary. I started out with typical import such as pandas, numpy, matplotlib and seaborn and later on I added specific parts of nltk and sklearn libraries for text analysis and predictions, respectively.
Next up were some common initial steps and first glances at the dataset. This was achieved with ordinary pandas functions (or functions of the pandas DataFrame object) such as read_csv, shape, head, info etc.
After few looks, I found out that the country and the city of each hotel are easily accessible from the hotel address column. Nevertheless, it occurred to me, that each country is represented only by one city, therefore the city column was meaningless and was dropped in addition to other columns, such as the hotel address, lattitude or longitude.
In the picture, you can see how my dataset looked like after these first steps. The data contained zero missing values, which made the initial data cleaning a lot easier, than it would be in most real-life tasks.

Country analysis

The next step was analysis from the perspective of countries, both country of the hotel and country of each reviewer.
However, there was a massive amount of unique countries in the reviewer country column. Therefore, I have decided to only look at the most frequent ones. For this purpose, I have created two functions; one allowed me to drop countries with value counts below a given threshold. The second one enabled me to only look at n most frequent countries.

Right from the start, I noticed a few things.
The United Kingdom was by far the most visited country as well as the country with the most reviewers.
I also found out that the country of each reviewer mattered quite a lot (as opposed to the country of each hotel) as there was a difference of more than one whole review point between the USA (country with the most positive feedback) and Iran (country with the lowest average review score).

In the following picture you can see the preferred country for reviewers coming from the 4 most frequent countries. It is not very surprising that people from the UK are far more likely to visit the UK than the other 5 countries.

Apart from this, I also checked whether the reviewers had given better ratings to hotels in their country of origin to any extent. However, I have not found any such relation.

Individual hotel analysis

After the country analysis I went on to analyse the hotels individually based on their name. That way, I found out, that hotel “Ritz Paris” is the best European hotel with the average score of 9.8, while “Hotel Liberty” is the worst one with 5.2.

This analysis led me to a very interesting idea of examining the extremes and their relation to the country of each hotel. This enabled me to compare the individual countries based on their occurrence in the top 50 or worst 50 (using the overall country distribution to see the differences).

Time analysis

Next up was the time analysis. This step was quite exciting for me as it incorporated working with time data in pandas, which can be a bit of a challenge from time to time. After some experiments I managed to create functions that would show the score evolution for the individual hotels based on their name.

The main problem was that not every hotel had the necessary amount of reviews to form such graph and as the reviews could range from 1 to 10, the graph might not tell the story very well. Therefore, I normalized the outliers and filled the blank spaces with the average score of the surrounding reviews. That way, I obtained way less “spiky” chart.

After I had created the function for the individual hotels, I realised that it would be great to be able to compare the score evolution for different hotels in time. I adapted the function a little and the result enabled me to enter a list of 5 different hotel names to compare.

Text analysis for tags

The following step was quite difficult but also one of the most interesting ones. Each review had a tag which would tell us, whether the person had a pet on the vacation, who did he or she travel with etc.

The first goal was to divide these tags into the individual features. This was the hard part as not all the reviews contained all tags, therefore it was quite a challenge to dig the information from some of the reviews.

As it came out, whether or not the person took a pet or was on a business trip rather than a vacation did not matter as much. However, the insight into the combination of travel group and length of stay and their combined effect on the average rating was quite interesting. (Note: The 2 highest average scores, both in “Travelers with friends”, namely 10-15 and 15+ days, both had only 1 or 2 occurrences, therefore cannot be considered of any value.)

Text analysis for reviews

And now the longest and probably the most insightful part. Content analysis of the text for each review.

The dataset was made such that each review had a “positive” and “negative” part. This enabled me to examine these individually and as it turned out, that was quite an important step, as each part (positive and negative) had its keywords. Apart from that, the number of words in each part (or their ratio) was a very interesting measure.

As the length of these parts was a predefined feature, it felt only natural to start with the inspection of that. In the following picture you may see the relation between the average score of a hotel and the average length of its positive and negative reviews.

After this step, the actual text analysis could start. Using CountVectorizer function from the sklearn package, I managed to find the most impactful keywords of both positive and negative reviews.
Four of these were chosen for each of them and in addition to that, I added “Everything” and “Nothing” as I had noticed that many reviewers tend to use these keywords in both parts of their review.
In the end, I had 10 keywords (4 for positive reviews, 4 for negative reviews, “Everything” and “Nothing”) and 12 new columns, respectively. For instance one new column would be something like ““Good location” mentioned in positive review”. As you could tell, these columns were either 1 or 0 and this actual example also incorporated words such as “perfect” or “excellent” in addition to location.
The result of all this was the following graph for positive reviews:

As we can see, staff was very important to the reviewers. Apart from that, putting “nothing” in their negative review or “everything” in their positive review usually led to higher scores (which was quite expected).

The following chart shows the results for negative reviews:

As we can see, all the keywords in negative reviews have quite massive impact. The occurrence (or absence, respectively) of a certain word in a reviewers negative review means a difference of up to 2 score points on average, which is huge.

Correlation and predictions

Last but not least was the inspection of correlation. In the following picture you can see the correlation vector for reviewer score. It shows the 10 most impactful features (or rather the 10 with highest correlation, to be correct). We can see that the ratio of lengths of positive and negative review parts was the most correlated one.

Multiple regression algorithms were used to try and predict the reviewer score. Linear regression and Random forest regressor both did quite well, however, they were outperformed by the Histogram gradient boosting regressor with the root of mean squared error at 0.728.

Hypotheses after examination

  • Length of negative (positive) reviews did have an impact; a feature of their mutual ratio has been engineered.
  • Date of each review was not impactful, however in some months the individual hotels show similar review average pattern.
  • Reviewer Nationality seemed impactful, nevertheless it is hard to say whether the reviewers from some countries tend to rate better in general or just pick better hotels.
  • On average, there was not much of a difference between the individual hotel countries. However, some countries performed much better in terms of the extreme cases (top and worst 50 hotels).
  • Apart from the increased number of reviewers, there is no correlation betweeen reviewer score and corresponding country of the reviewer and his or her hotel.
  • Certain keywords in both negative and positive review have had a meaningful impact on the reviewer score, e.g. “air conditioning” or “nothing” in reviewers negative review.
  • Specific features have been extracted from tags of each review; these features have been shown to have some effect on the reviewer score.

Possible next steps

  • Re-shaping the target variable into ordinal categorical one; this would allow me to inspect the data in terms of multivariate classification.
  • Analysing the data from the perspective of clustering; that would enable us to dive deep into the reviewers, their demands and behaviour; this may have a massive impact on the business applicability of the project
